Why Mental Health

To build the foundations of a mentally sane Africa.

It’s been predicted by the World Health Organization (WHO), that, by the end of 2020, depression would be the leading contributor to the global burden of diseases, right behind Ischemic heart diseases. Worldwide, 121 million people experience a depressive episode within a 12 month period. Then there is psychosis, anxiety disorders, ADHD, autism and other behavior disorders. Yet, our leaders have not done enough to implement mental health policies that would cater for the millions of young people in need of treatment.

Stigma

What comes to mind when we hear the word “MENTAL” is mad. So for most people, to have a mental illness is to be nearing madness. This lie has created a lot of stigma and shame for those who are experiencing symptoms of a mental illness for fear of being judged as “mad” or “weak”.

Ignorance

In most African languages there no words for mental illnesses like “depression” or “anxiety”. Sadly our society has refused to acknowledge the existence of these conditions but for the thousands of young people who committed suicide because of their insufferable conditions, they couldn’t be more real. Just like headaches, everyone can have a mental illness, so everyone should know about them.

Myth

Unfortunately, the ignorance surrounding mental illnesses have allowed the dominance of popular but mistaken beliefs about their nature and treatment. Mental illnesses cam are NOT caused by spirits or demons and they can be treated.

Key Facts

One in six people are aged 10–19 years.

Globally, depression is one of the leading causes of illness and disability among adolescents.

Half of all mental health conditions start by 14 years of age but most cases are undetected and untreated.

Mental health conditions account for 16% of the global burden of disease and injury in people aged 10–19 years.

African countries dedicate on average less than 1% of their already small health budgets to mental health.

Suicide is the third leading cause of death in 15-19-year-olds.

There is 1 psychologist to 1,000,000 people in Africa

The consequences of not addressing adolescent mental health conditions extend to adulthood, impairing both physical and mental health and limiting opportunities to lead fulfilling lives as adults.
